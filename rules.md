# Rules: Professional Investigative Language Standards

## Principle: Meaning = Use

Professional language gets its meaning from how it is used in investigative practice. We evaluate whether you are using professional terms correctly.

---

## Core Distinctions (What You Cannot Confuse)

These distinctions define the professional language game. Violating them means your language is not professional.

### 1. Observation ≠ Interpretation

**Observation:** What you directly perceived or documented.  
**Interpretation:** What you concluded about what you perceived.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "The claimant appeared fraudulent."
- "Appeared fraudulent" is interpretation (conclusion about intent)
- You observed something, but fraud = intent, which is not observable

✅ **CORRECT:** "The claimant made three contradictory statements about when the loss occurred. [Facts]. This contradiction is noteworthy. Additional evidence is needed to determine whether this is error or intentional."

**Rule:** Always report observation first. Then explain your interpretation. Then identify what evidence gap remains.

---

### 2. Evidence ≠ Conclusion

**Evidence:** Facts, documents, testimony, expert findings that support a conclusion.  
**Conclusion:** What you decided the evidence means.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "The evidence proves the claimant committed fraud."
- Evidence doesn't "prove" anything by itself
- Your job is to report evidence; decision-makers decide what it means

✅ **CORRECT:** "Evidence collected: [list]. This evidence is consistent with [interpretation]. However, [alternative interpretation] is also consistent with this evidence. The following evidence would distinguish between these: [list]."

**Rule:** Report what you found. Explain what it could mean. Identify what gaps remain.

---

### 3. Suspicion ≠ Evidence

**Suspicion:** Your gut feeling something is wrong.  
**Evidence:** Observable, documented facts.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "I suspected the loss report was inflated, and I determined it was fraud."
- Suspicion is not evidence
- You need documentation to support your determination

✅ **CORRECT:** "The loss report claimed [amount]. My investigation found [documented facts]. Discrepancy: [specific difference]. This suggests possible overstatement. Additional evidence is needed."

**Rule:** Never use suspicion as the basis for a finding. Document what you observed.

---

### 4. Possibility ≠ Probability ≠ Proof

**Possibility:** It could happen.  
**Probability:** It probably happened (likelihood based on evidence).  
**Proof:** Overwhelming evidence it definitely happened (legal standard).

❌ **VIOLATION:** "Fraud is possible."
- Starts investigation; doesn't conclude it
- Everything is possible

❌ **VIOLATION:** "It's likely fraud."
- Probability without documented basis

✅ **CORRECT:** "Based on [documented facts], fraud appears [probable/unlikely]. Evidence supporting this: [facts]. Alternative explanations: [list]. Evidence distinguishing between them: [needed evidence]."

**Rule:** Be precise about level of certainty. Always tie it to documented facts.

---

### 5. Intent ≠ Motive

**Intent:** Deliberate choice to do something.  
**Motive:** Reason for having that intent.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "The claimant's financial need proves intent to commit fraud."
- Motive (need money) is not the same as intent (deliberately lied)

✅ **CORRECT:** "The claimant's financial records show [facts]. Financial motive exists: [documented]. However, financial motive alone does not establish intent to commit fraud. Evidence of deliberate misrepresentation would be [specific facts]."

**Rule:** Never infer intent from motive. Document deliberate deception, not financial pressure.

---

### 6. Contradiction ≠ Deception

**Contradiction:** Two statements that don't match.  
**Deception:** Intentional false statement.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "The contradictory statements prove the claimant was being deceptive."
- Contradiction is observable; intent is not
- Could be error, memory lapse, or misunderstanding

✅ **CORRECT:** "Statement 1 [documented]. Statement 2 [documented]. Contradiction: [specific difference]. This inconsistency is noteworthy. It could indicate [possibilities]. Additional evidence to determine intent: [what would distinguish between error and deception]."

**Rule:** Identify contradictions as facts. Do not assume intent. Document what would prove intent.

---

### 7. Recommendation ≠ Decision

**Recommendation:** What you suggest the decision-maker consider.  
**Decision:** What the decision-maker actually decides about coverage/liability.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "I recommend we deny this claim."
- That's a coverage decision, not your role
- You recommend findings, not coverage outcomes

✅ **CORRECT:** "Findings: [documented facts]. Coverage question: Does this evidence satisfy the policy requirements for [specific coverage section]? Recommended next step: Legal review of policy interpretation."

**Rule:** Report findings. Recommend what decision-makers should review. Do not make coverage or liability decisions.

---

### 8. Finding ≠ Allegation

**Finding:** Fact you have documented and verified.  
**Allegation:** Claim that has not yet been verified.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "Finding: The claimant committed fraud."
- You have evidence of fraud? Then say what evidence.
- Otherwise it's an allegation, not a finding.

✅ **CORRECT:** "Allegation: Claimant statements at [time] claimed [fact]. Finding: Investigation found [documented evidence]. Inconsistency indicates [possible explanation]. Status: [verified/unverified]."

**Rule:** Distinguish what was alleged from what you found. Label each appropriately.

---

### 9. Missing Evidence ≠ Evidence of Concealment

**Missing Evidence:** You couldn't find something you looked for.  
**Evidence of Concealment:** Deliberate effort to hide something.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "The claimant refused to provide receipts, which proves they have something to hide."
- Missing evidence is not proof of concealment
- Could be disorganization, loss, or legitimate reason

✅ **CORRECT:** "Receipts were requested on [date]. Response: [what claimant provided]. Documentation sought but not located: [specific records]. This is noteworthy because [why it matters]. Evidence of deliberate concealment would be [specific facts]."

**Rule:** Distinguish between "could not find" and "someone is hiding." Document the difference.

---

### 10. Policy Language ≠ Interpretation

**Policy Language:** What the policy actually says (quoted or precisely cited).  
**Interpretation:** What you think it means.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "The policy clearly excludes this loss because..."
- Does the policy actually say that? Or are you interpreting?

✅ **CORRECT:** "Policy Section [cite] states: '[quote exact language].' This appears to [your interpretation], because [reasoning]. However, [alternative interpretation] is also possible. Legal review recommended."

**Rule:** Always distinguish policy language from your interpretation. Quote when possible. Note ambiguities.

---

### 11. Source Attribution Required

**Every material fact must have a source.**

- Observation (what you saw)
- Document (what you read)
- Testimony (who told you)
- Expert (who analyzed it)

❌ **VIOLATION:** "The claimant made inconsistent statements."
- When? To whom? What exactly did they say?

✅ **CORRECT:** "Claimant statement on [date] to [person]: '[quote or precise paraphrase].' Later statement on [date] to [person]: '[quote].' Inconsistency: [specific difference]."

**Rule:** Never make a statement of fact without identifying its source.

---

### 12. Finding ≠ Policy Interpretation (NEW: 2026-07-22)

**Every significant finding must map to specific policy language.**

Investigators report findings. Claims adjusters interpret policy. The bridge between them must be explicit.

❌ **VIOLATION:** "The dwelling suffered water damage. The claim is covered."
- Which coverage? (A, B, or C)
- Which peril? (Policy lists specific perils)
- Which exclusions apply?
- No policy language cited

✅ **CORRECT:** "The insured reported water damage from burst hot water heater (Coverage A - Dwelling). Policy Peril #12 covers 'Sudden and Accidental Water Damage from domestic appliances.' Investigation verified: (1) water source is domestic appliance; (2) policy exclusions for below-ground water, backed-up water, and fungus do not apply; (3) insured gave prompt notice and cooperated fully. For adjuster: Findings support coverage under Coverage A, Peril #12."

**Rule:** Identify which coverage applies. Cite the specific peril or cause of loss. Address relevant exclusions. Verify special limits apply. Confirm policy conditions (prompt notice, cooperation, proof of loss, etc.). Let the claims adjuster make the coverage decision.

**Reference:** See `reference/texas-ho3-policy-structured.md` (actual policy language) and `reference/policy-alignment-integration.md` (how to integrate policy into reports).

## How We Apply These Rules

### Layer 1: Source Attribution
Does every fact have a source? If not, flag it first.

### Layer 2: Observation vs Interpretation
Are observations separated from conclusions? If blurred, flag it.

### Layer 3: Evidence Standards
Are only documented facts used as evidence? If speculation or suspicion, flag it.

### Layer 4: Professional Boundaries
Has the investigator stayed in role (reporting facts, not making decisions)? If not, flag it.

### Layer 5: Language Precision
Is professional terminology used correctly? If misused, flag it.

## Authority for These Rules

These rules come from **four foundational Texas cases:**
- **Arnold v. National County** — Establishes "reasonable documented basis" standard for investigative findings
- **Stoker v. State Farm** — Establishes that coverage denial requires a valid reason
- **Giles v. State Farm** — Establishes requirements for timely payment and clear liability
- **Menchaca v. State Farm** — Establishes requirements for reasonable investigation and clear explanation

Plus:
- **Professional standards:** ACFE (Certified Fraud Examiner) investigative best practices
- **Linguistic theory:** Wittgenstein (meaning = use in professional practice)
- **Insurance policy:** Actual HO-3 homeowners policy language and provisions
- **State law:** Texas Insurance Code requirements

---

## Severity Levels

**CRITICAL:** Violates professional boundaries (e.g., makes coverage decision, confuses intent with motive in fraud finding)

**HIGH:** Violates evidence standards (e.g., conclusion exceeds evidence, suspicion presented as fact)

**MEDIUM:** Violates clarity standards (e.g., unclear traceability, vague terminology)

**LOW:** Style/organization (e.g., could be clearer, but not professionally wrong)

## Bottom Line

Professional investigative language has rules. Your job as an investigator is to follow them.

Our job as your editor is to show you when you haven't.

When we flag something, we're not saying you're wrong. We're saying: "This language doesn't follow the professional rules. Here's the rule. Here's how to improve it."

The goal is to teach you so that next time, you catch it yourself.
