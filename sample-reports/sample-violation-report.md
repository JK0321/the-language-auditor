# Sample Violation Report: What NOT to Do

This is an example of an investigative report with multiple professional language violations.

We've marked the violations so you can see what the The Language Auditor would flag.

---

## Sample Report: Water Damage Claim

**Date:** January 18, 2026  
**Claim:** WL-2026-001234  
**Insured:** John Smith  
**Type:** Homeowners - Water Damage

---

### Investigation Summary

The insured, John Smith, filed a claim for water damage allegedly caused by a sprinkler malfunction in the basement on January 14. Our investigation found several concerning items that suggest possible fraud. The claimant appears to be deliberately overstating the loss to collect more money from the insurance company.

**[VIOLATION 1: Observation ≠ Interpretation + Intent ≠ Evidence]**
- "Appears to be deliberately overstating" = interpretation and assumed intent
- Based on what specific evidence? 
- Documented facts would be clearer.

---

### Loss Timeline

The claimant reported the loss on January 16, two days after it allegedly occurred. This suspicious delay indicates the claimant had time to stage the loss and make up a story.

**[VIOLATION 2: Contradiction ≠ Deception + Assumption of Intent]**
- A two-day delay could mean many things
- Why is it "suspicious"? 
- No evidence of staging is presented
- Inferring intent from timing lacks support

**[VIOLATION 3: Missing Sources]**
- When exactly did the claimant report? To whom?
- What are the details of the report?

---

### Property Inspection

I inspected the property on January 18. Water damage was evident in the basement. The claimant claimed the loss was $50,000, but my inspection indicated it was really only about $25,000. This massive discrepancy suggests fraud.

**[VIOLATION 4: Source Attribution Missing]**
- How did you determine $25,000? What method?
- Does claimant estimate of $50,000 come from formal assessment or casual estimate?
- What documentation supports each number?

**[VIOLATION 5: Evidence ≠ Conclusion]**
- A discrepancy between claimant's estimate and your assessment doesn't "suggest fraud"
- Claimants often overestimate losses
- Intentional fraud requires evidence of deliberate deception, not just overestimation

---

### Sprinkler System

The sprinkler head that allegedly burst has not been located or inspected. This missing evidence proves the claimant is concealing the source of the water damage and probably caused it deliberately.

**[VIOLATION 6: Missing Evidence ≠ Evidence of Concealment]**
- Missing evidence is not evidence of concealment
- Where did you look for the sprinkler head?
- Why wasn't it found? (Disposal, remodeling, insurance adjuster removed it?)
- "Probably caused it deliberately" is speculation, not evidence

**[VIOLATION 7: Source Attribution]**
- Who determined the sprinkler head "cannot be located"?
- What search efforts were made?

---

### Claimant Interview

I spoke with the claimant about the loss. His story didn't make sense. He said the sprinkler burst on the evening of January 14, but he didn't notice until the next morning. This obviously shows he knew what happened and was just making up a convenient story.

**[VIOLATION 8: Observation ≠ Interpretation + Intent]**
- "His story didn't make sense" = your interpretation
- What specifically didn't make sense?
- The delay in discovering a basement loss overnight is normal, not suspicious

**[VIOLATION 9: Source Attribution]**
- What exactly did the claimant say? Quote it.
- When did he discover the loss? What time?
- What was he doing when he discovered it?

**[VIOLATION 10: Assumption of Intent]**
- "He knew what happened and was just making up a story" = assumption
- What evidence shows deliberate fabrication vs. honest error about timeline?

---

### Conclusion

Based on my investigation, I recommend we deny this claim because the insured is clearly committing insurance fraud. The missing sprinkler head, the suspicious delay, and the inflated estimate all prove this was a staged loss. I recommend immediate claim denial and referral to the fraud department.

**[VIOLATION 11: Investigator ≠ Decision-Maker]**
- You recommend claim denial = coverage decision (that's not your role)
- Your role: Report findings. Decision-maker determines coverage.

**[VIOLATION 12: Evidence ≠ Conclusion]**
- "Clearly committing insurance fraud" = conclusion that exceeds presented evidence
- You've presented suspicions and discrepancies, not proof of fraud

**[VIOLATION 13: No Alternative Explanations]**
- No consideration of innocent explanations for the observations
- No evidence gaps identified
- No additional facts that would distinguish fraud from error

## What This Report Should Have Looked Like

### Revised Investigation Summary

**Date:** January 18, 2026  
**Claim:** WL-2026-001234  
**Insured:** John Smith  
**Type:** Homeowners - Water Damage

### Loss Timeline

Claim reported: January 16, 2026 (phone call, documented by adjuster [name])

Insured statement (January 16 call): Loss discovered on morning of January 15; water damage first noticed when checking basement for unrelated reason.

**Analysis:** Two-day delay between loss occurrence and discovery is within normal range for basement damage (not typically in frequently-used area).

---

### Damage Assessment

**Insured's estimate (from claim form):** $50,000

**My inspection findings (January 18):**
- Water damage location: East wall, north corner, height [description]
- Affected materials: Carpet, drywall, stored items [list]
- Sprinkler head location: [describe location in ceiling]
- Sprinkler head condition: [observed condition]

**Professional assessment:** Based on [assessment method], estimated cost of repair/replacement: $[amount]

**Discrepancy:** Between insured's estimate and professional assessment is $[amount].

**Possible explanations:**
1. Insured's estimate included items outside policy coverage
2. Insured overestimated repair costs
3. Insured's estimate included loss-of-use costs
4. My assessment missed damages in areas not fully visible

**Evidence to distinguish:** Review of [specific items]. Comparison with [similar claims]. Follow-up inspection of [specific areas].

---

### Sprinkler System Investigation

**Observation:** Sprinkler head that allegedly burst is not visible in current location.

**Search conducted:** [specific actions taken]

**Possible explanations:**
1. Sprinkler head was removed during cleanup
2. Sprinkler head displacement due to water pressure
3. Sprinkler head was taken as evidence or sample
4. Different pipe section actually ruptured

**Evidence to clarify:** Photograph of plumbing prior to cleanup. Claimant interview about cleanup steps. Plumber's assessment of system condition.

---

### Claimant Interview

**Date:** January 16, 2026  
**Method:** Telephone  
**Documented by:** [your name]

Claimant's account:
- "On evening of January 14, around 10 PM, sprinkler system in basement turned on automatically"
- "Water began pooling near east wall"
- "I checked basement the next morning and discovered the extent of damage"
- "I called adjuster at [time] on January 16"

**Notes on account:**
- Timeline is consistent with sprinkler timer operation
- Delay in discovery is reasonable for basement damage
- Claimant was cooperative; provided details without prompting

**Follow-up questions:** Where is the water shutoff? Was system shut off immediately? What records exist for sprinkler installation/maintenance?

---

### Conclusion & Recommendation

**Findings:**
- Water damage documented on [date] at [location]
- Apparent source: Sprinkler system malfunction
- Damage assessment: [amount] (professional estimate)
- Discrepancy with claimant's estimate: [amount and possible causes]
- Equipment inspection: Sprinkler head not located [possible reasons]

**Outstanding questions:**
- [Specific facts that would clarify the loss]

**Recommended next step:**
- Coverage analysis by [appropriate role] regarding whether loss meets policy requirements for covered water damage
- Follow-up inspection by plumber if [specific condition]
- Additional documentation request: [specific items]

## What's Different?

✅ **Better version:**
- States facts specifically
- Distinguishes observation from interpretation
- Considers alternative explanations
- Identifies evidence gaps
- Stays in investigator role (reports findings; doesn't make decisions)
- Uses precise language
- Every claim is sourced and documented

❌ **Original version:**
- Makes conclusions without supporting facts
- Assumes intent from circumstance
- Ignores alternatives
- Missing sources and documentation
- Crosses into decision-making
- Uses vague language
- "Obviously" and "clearly" without evidence

---

## Lesson

The better version would be strengthened by:
1. Specific documents referenced (form numbers, dates)
2. Formal assessment method (cost estimate, comparable losses)
3. Plumber's technical assessment
4. Additional investigation to distinguish explanations

But it's already professional because it:
- ✅ Reports what was found
- ✅ Explains why it matters
- ✅ Identifies gaps
- ✅ Lets decision-makers decide coverage

This is the standard the The Language Auditor checks for.
