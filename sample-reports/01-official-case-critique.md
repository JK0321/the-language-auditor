## Test Report: Water Damage Claim

**Date:** January 18, 2026  
**Claim:** WL-2026-001234  
**Insured:** John Smith  
**Type:** Homeowners - Water Damage  
**Submitted by:** Jane Investigator

---

### Investigation Summary

On January 16, the insured reported a water loss that occurred on the evening of January 14. The insured claimed a sprinkler system malfunction caused water damage to the basement. Our inspection on January 18 found significant water damage consistent with the reported cause. The insured's damage estimate of $50,000 appears overstated based on professional assessment of $28,000.

---

### Loss Timeline

- **January 14, 10:15 PM:** Insured states sprinkler system activated automatically
- **January 15, 8:00 AM:** Insured discovered water damage while checking basement
- **January 16, 2:30 PM:** Loss reported to adjuster (phone call documented in system)
- **January 18, 10:00 AM:** Investigator inspection conducted

---

### Property Inspection Findings

**Date:** January 18, 2026  
**Inspector:** Jane Investigator  
**Weather:** Clear; no recent rain

**Damage observed:**
- Water damage on east wall of basement, extending approximately 4 feet up from floor
- Water line visible on concrete; indicates water depth of approximately 18-24 inches
- Carpet and stored items (boxes, furniture) affected
- Drywall damage on lower portion of walls

**Sprinkler system:**
- Sprinkler head located in ceiling approximately 8 feet from damaged wall
- Head appears intact; no visible damage
- System control unit shows timer set for 10:00 PM automatic activation
- No visible water around control unit or supply lines

**Documentation:** Photographs taken of damage extent, water lines, and sprinkler system

---

### Damage Assessment

**Insured's estimate (from claim form):** $50,000

**Professional assessment (based on comparable water damage claims):** $28,000

**Discrepancy:** $22,000

**Analysis of discrepancy:**
Insured's estimate may include:
- Replacement of contents (furniture, stored items) that may be covered under separate endorsement
- Temporary lodging costs (if displacement required)
- Items outside policy coverage area

Professional assessment based on documented damage to structure and permanently damaged items in loss area.

---

### Claimant Interview

**Date:** January 16, 2026 (phone)  
**Duration:** Approximately 15 minutes  
**Documented by:** Claims Adjuster [name]

**Claimant account (from adjuster notes):**
- "I was watching television around 10 PM when I heard water running"
- "I went downstairs and found water coming from the ceiling"
- "I turned off the main water valve immediately"
- "The next morning I checked the basement and saw the full extent of damage"

**Follow-up questions asked:**
- When did you last check basement before the incident? "Probably a week before"
- Do you have maintenance records for sprinkler system? "Not with me; I'll look"
- Any previous issues with water in basement? "Never"

**Note from adjuster:** Insured was cooperative; provided information without hesitation

---

### Outstanding Items

1. Maintenance records for sprinkler system (requested from insured)
2. Comparison inspection by sprinkler contractor (to determine if malfunction or normal operation)
3. Photos from insured (if taken before damage assessment)

---

### Recommendation

Coverage analysis recommended regarding:
- Extent of coverage for water damage from internal malfunction
- Applicability of replacement cost vs actual cash value
- Deductible application

## What the Editor Would Flag

When you run this report through the The Language Auditor, here's what it catches:

### FINDINGS SUMMARY

**Total flags:** 3  
**Critical:** 0 | **High:** 1 | **Medium:** 2 | **Low:** 0

---

### FLAG #1: Missing Evidence Detail

**Location:** Property Inspection section, "Sprinkler system" paragraph  
**Severity:** MEDIUM  
**Rule Violated:** Source Attribution (Rule #11)

**Original text:**
"Head appears intact; no visible damage"

**Issue:**
This is conclusory. What specific observations support "appears intact"? What would "damage" look like?

**Explanation:**
The reader can't verify your conclusion without knowing what you actually observed. "Intact" needs supporting detail.

**Authority:** Professional investigative standards; legal defensibility

**Recommendation:**
"Head appears intact: threads visible and intact, metal housing undamaged, no cracks or dents visible. No water spray pattern observed when system tested. Spray pattern would indicate nozzle damage."

---

### FLAG #2: Incomplete Discrepancy Analysis

**Location:** Damage Assessment section, "Analysis of discrepancy" paragraph  
**Severity:** MEDIUM  
**Rule Violated:** Evidence ≠ Conclusion (Rule #2) + Alternative Hypotheses missing

**Original text:**
"Insured's estimate may include: Replacement of contents... Professional assessment based on documented damage..."

**Issue:**
You identified a $22,000 discrepancy but didn't fully distinguish between these possibilities:
1. Insured overestimated
2. Insured used different valuation method
3. Your assessment missed damage areas
4. Insured's estimate legitimately includes items outside your scope

**Explanation:**
A discrepancy is observable. But the cause isn't always obvious. Presenting the discrepancy without resolving which explanation applies leaves the decision-maker uncertain.

**Authority:** Professional investigative standards; Arnold standard (reasonable documented basis)

**Recommendation:**
"Discrepancy analysis:
- Insured's estimate: $50,000 [based on: claim form date X, method unknown]
- Professional assessment: $28,000 [based on: comparable claims database, structural damage measurement]
- Difference: $22,000

Possible explanations:
1. Contents coverage vs structural coverage difference
2. Insured used contractor estimate; I used database comparison
3. Insured included temporary housing/lodging costs

To distinguish between these:
- Request insured's itemized breakdown (what items = $50K?)
- Compare line items to professional assessment
- Clarify policy coverage for contents vs structure"

---

### FLAG #3: Incomplete Claimant Interview Documentation

**Location:** Claimant Interview section, "Follow-up questions" paragraph  
**Severity:** LOW  
**Rule Violated:** Source Attribution (Rule #11)

**Original text:**
"Follow-up questions asked:
- When did you last check basement before the incident? "Probably a week before"
- Do you have maintenance records for sprinkler system? "Not with me; I'll look""

**Issue:**
Good detail. But what was the actual response to "maintenance records"? Did claimant provide them? Date they'd be provided? Status?

**Explanation:**
You asked the question but didn't document the outcome. Readers can't tell if this is still outstanding or resolved.

**Authority:** Professional documentation standards

**Recommendation:**
"Follow-up questions asked:
- When did you last check basement before incident? Claimant stated: 'Probably a week before' [Note: informal timeline; consider requesting documentation of last maintenance visit]
- Maintenance records for sprinkler system? Claimant stated: 'Not with me; I'll look' Status: [Pending / Received on X date / Claimant unable to locate]"

---

## Positive Observations (What the Editor Accepts)

✅ **Well done:**
- Clear timeline with specific dates and times
- Documented source for claimant account (adjuster notes)
- Specific damage measurements (water depth, wall height)
- Photographic documentation noted
- Recognition of outstanding items and follow-up needed
- Appropriate recommendation for decision-maker review

✅ **Professional language:**
- Stays in investigator role (reports findings; doesn't recommend coverage decision)
- Distinguishes observation from conclusion ("appears intact" is stated as observation)
- Acknowledges alternatives (possible reasons for discrepancy)
- Identifies information gaps (maintenance records, sprinkler contractor assessment)

---

## How the Report Would Look Revised

The three flags above are easy to fix. The report would change from good to excellent with these additions:

1. **Sprinkler head details:** Add specific observations (threads visible, no cracks, spray pattern tested)
2. **Discrepancy breakdown:** Add methodology differences and how to distinguish between them
3. **Interview status:** Note whether maintenance records were received and when

**Revised version would:**
- ✅ Be more detailed without being longer
- ✅ Support decision-making with clearer analysis
- ✅ Have fewer questions from decision-makers
- ✅ Meet Arnold standard of "reasonable documented basis"
