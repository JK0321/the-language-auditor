# Examples: Good Critique vs Bad Critique

This document shows what we mean by specific, actionable feedback.

**Primary Example:** We use the official Water Damage investigation (see `sample-reports/00-water-damage-official.md`) throughout these examples. This is a real, professionally-formatted report showing how the editor works on actual investigative writing.

---

## Example 1: Generic Feedback (WHAT WE DON'T DO)

### Bad Critique Example

**Original report excerpt:**
"The claimant's statement about the loss timing was inconsistent with her prior statement. This suggests possible fraud."

**Bad critique (generic):**
"Consider strengthening your findings. The use of language here is unclear. Try to be more specific about what you found."

**Why this fails:**
- Generic ("unclear")
- Doesn't tell the writer what to fix
- Doesn't explain the actual rule violation
- Doesn't reference any standard

### Specific Critique (WHAT WE DO)

**Same original excerpt:**
"The claimant's statement about the loss timing was inconsistent with her prior statement. This suggests possible fraud."

**Specific critique:**

**FLAG: Contradiction ≠ Deception**
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Location:** Paragraph 3, sentence 2-3
- **Rule violated:** Contradiction ≠ Deception (Professional Language Game Rule #6)

**Explanation:**
You observed an inconsistency (that's good—report it). You then concluded "suggests possible fraud" (that's interpretation of intent).

The rule: A contradiction is observable and reportable. But "fraud" means intentional deception. Intent is not observable. You cannot infer intent from contradiction alone.

**Authority:** Professional investigative standards require distinguishing observable facts (contradiction) from conclusions about intent (fraud determination).

**Recommendation:**
Rewrite as: "Statement 1 [date, person]: claimant said loss occurred on [date]. Statement 2 [date, person]: claimant said loss occurred on [different date]. This inconsistency is noteworthy.

Possible explanations: (1) Memory error, (2) Misunderstanding the question, (3) Deliberate misstatement. 

Evidence distinguishing between these explanations:
- Witness confirmation of actual loss date?
- Timeline of other documented events?
- Claimant's explanation for the discrepancy?"

## Example 2: The "Appears" Problem

### Original Report Excerpt

"The claimant appears to have committed fraud based on the timeline inconsistency."

### Specific Critique

**FLAG: Observation ≠ Interpretation + Intent ≠ Motive**
- **Severity:** CRITICAL
- **Location:** Paragraph 2, final sentence
- **Rules violated:** 
  - Observation ≠ Interpretation (Rule #1)
  - Intent ≠ Motive (Rule #5)

**Explanation:**
"Appears to have committed" is a conclusion (interpretation of intent). Your job is to report what you observed, not conclude about intent.

What you actually observed: A timeline discrepancy. That's reportable.

What you concluded: Fraud. That requires evidence of deliberate deception, not just inconsistency.

The rule: Observations are facts. Interpretations are conclusions. Fraud is an intent-based conclusion that requires proof of deliberate false statement.

**Authority:** Texas case law (Arnold v. National County—reasonable documented basis; Stoker v. State Farm—coverage denial requires valid reason; Giles v. State Farm—timely payment and clear liability; Menchaca v. State Farm—reasonable investigation and clear explanation); Professional Language Game (observation ≠ interpretation).

**Recommendation:**
Rewrite as: "Timeline analysis: 
- Claimant stated loss occurred [date A]
- Loss documentation shows [date B]  
- Discrepancy: [specific difference]

This inconsistency requires explanation. Possible causes:
1. Claimant memory error (timeline is difficult to recall)
2. Claimant misunderstood the question
3. Deliberate misstatement

Evidence to distinguish between these:
- Does claimant have explanation for discrepancy? [document response]
- Are there independent witnesses to actual loss date?
- Is claimant's account consistent with other documented facts?"

## Example 3: The Missing Authority Problem

### Original Report Excerpt

"Based on the policy, this loss is not covered because the damage exceeds what any reasonable insured would expect to claim."

### Specific Critique

**FLAG: Policy Language ≠ Interpretation**
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Location:** Paragraph 4, sentence 1
- **Rule violated:** Policy Language ≠ Interpretation (Rule #10)

**Explanation:**
You stated a coverage conclusion without quoting the actual policy language that supports it.

"Exceeds what a reasonable insured would expect to claim" is your interpretation, not the policy's language.

What policy section actually says this? Quote it precisely. Or is this your inference?

**Authority:** Professional standards require precise policy citation. Ambiguities should be identified and escalated for legal review.

**Recommendation:**
Rewrite as: "Coverage analysis:
The policy Section [X] states: '[exact quote of exclusion or limitation]'

This appears to exclude [your interpretation] because [reasoning].

However, [alternative interpretation] is also consistent with this language.

Policy language that would clearly support your interpretation: [what would need to be stated]

Recommended: Legal review of Section [X] interpretation."

## Example 4: The Source Attribution Problem

### Original Report Excerpt

"The insured made suspicious phone calls on the date of loss and was observed by neighbors acting strangely."

### Specific Critique

**FLAG: Source Attribution Missing**
- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Location:** Paragraph 3, sentence 1
- **Rule violated:** Source Attribution Required (Rule #11)

**Explanation:**
Every fact needs a source. You told us the insured made calls and acted strangely. But:
- Who told you about the phone calls? How do you know?
- Which neighbors? What exactly did they observe? When? To whom?
- What does "strangely" mean?

Without sources, these are allegations, not findings.

**Authority:** Professional investigative standards require documented source for every material fact. Documentation enables audit trail and legal defensibility.

**Recommendation:**
Rewrite as: "Timeline of [date]:
- Phone records [source: carrier documentation dated X] show insured made [number] calls during [time period]. Call recipients: [specific numbers/names]. Duration: [times].
- Neighbor statement [name, date, documented interview]: '[exact quote about what was observed]'
- Other witnesses: [list and what each observed]

Analysis: [what this means in context]"

## Example 5: Decision Boundary Violation

### Original Report Excerpt

"Based on my investigation, I recommend we deny this claim because the evidence clearly demonstrates the claimant is committing fraud."

### Specific Critique

**FLAG: Investigator ≠ Decision-Maker + Conclusion Exceeds Evidence**
- **Severity:** CRITICAL
- **Location:** Paragraph 7, sentence 1
- **Rules violated:**
  - Recommendation ≠ Decision (Rule #7)
  - Evidence ≠ Conclusion (Rule #2)

**Explanation:**
You made two mistakes in one sentence:

1. **Investigator role violation:** You recommended a coverage decision ("deny the claim"). That's the decision-maker's job (claims adjuster, manager, counsel). Your job is to report findings. Decision-makers decide what to do with your findings.

2. **Evidence standard violation:** You stated evidence "clearly demonstrates" fraud. That's a very high bar. What specific evidence reaches that level? If you have that evidence, what is it?

**Authority:** Professional standards and company SOP define investigator role as fact-finding, not decision-making. Legal standards for fraud require proof beyond reasonable doubt of deliberate deception.

**Recommendation:**
Rewrite as: "Findings from investigation:

Evidence supporting possible fraud:
[Documented facts, each sourced]

Evidence that contradicts or complicates this:
[Alternative explanations, each documented]

Outstanding evidence gaps:
[What would clarify the question]

Recommended next step: Coverage analysis by [appropriate role] regarding whether facts established meet policy exclusion criteria for [specific provision]."

## Example 6: Good Report (What We're Looking For)

### Sample Excerpt from a Report We'd Accept

"**Loss Timeline Investigation**

Claimant statement (form dated 1/15, received by adjuster 1/16):
- Loss discovered: evening of 1/14
- Cause: sprinkler malfunction
- Damage: water to entire basement

Property inspection (1/18, investigator notes):
- Water damage observed: [describe location, extent, water mark height]
- Sprinkler head damage: [describe specifics]
- Basement contents affected: [list]

Discrepancy identified:
- Claimant estimated damage at $X
- Actual documented damage (photos, receipts): $Y
- Difference: $[amount]

Possible explanations:
1. Claimant underestimated (common in sudden-loss situations)
2. Claimant's estimate included items outside policy coverage
3. Claimant's documented receipts are incomplete

Evidence distinguishing between these:
- Property inspection photos: [shows actual damage matches X or Y]
- Claimant receipts review: [shows covered vs uncovered items]
- Adjuster estimate: [professional valuation]

Recommendation for next step:
Legal review of coverage for items in dispute, and reconciliation of dollar amount based on documented damage assessment."

**Why this works:**
- ✅ Every fact is sourced
- ✅ Observations are separated from interpretations
- ✅ Conclusions are tied to documented evidence
- ✅ Alternatives are considered
- ✅ Gaps are identified
- ✅ Recommendation is for decision-maker consideration, not decision-making
- ✅ Language is precise and professional

## Bottom Line

**Bad critique:** "This is unclear. Be more specific."

**Good critique:** "You observed [fact]. You concluded [interpretation]. The rule is [named rule]. The authority is [source]. To make this professional, you could [specific revision]."

We provide the second kind.
