# Case Law Authority Index

This index maps the five foundational Texas insurance cases to the professional language rules in the The Language Auditor.

---

## Four Core Standards + Philosophy

All Texas insurance investigators must understand these four foundational cases. Together they define the rules that Wittgenstein's theory explains are already embedded in professional practice:

### 1. Arnold v. National County (1987)
**Standard:** Reasonable Documented Basis  
**Focus:** Quality of investigative report  
**Violation:** Report lacks facts, sources, or reasoning  
**File:** `01-arnold-standard.md`

### 2. República Ins v. Stoker (1995)
**Standard:** Valid Reason for Denial  
**Focus:** Validity of coverage determination  
**Violation:** Denial without valid contractual reason or documentation  
**File:** `02-stoker-standard.md`

### 3. Universe Life v. Giles (1997)
**Standard:** Reasonably Clear Liability + Timely Payment  
**Focus:** Claims handling speed and completion  
**Violation:** Delay in payment after liability is clear; unnecessary document requests  
**File:** `03-giles-standard.md`

### 4. USAA v. Menchaca (2018)
**Standard:** Reasonable Investigation + Clear Explanation + Timely Decision  
**Focus:** Insurance Code compliance  
**Violation:** Insufficient investigation; unclear explanation; untimely decision; policy misrepresentation  
**File:** `04-menchaca-standard.md`

### 5. State Farm Lloyds v. Nicolau (1997)
**Standard:** Investigative Methodology + Report Integrity  
**Focus:** Investigation quality and objectivity  
**Violation:** Inadequate investigation; failure to fairly evaluate conflicting evidence; refusal to reassess with new evidence  
**File:** `05-nicolau-standard.md`

## How They Connect to Professional Language Rules

### Rule #1: Observation ≠ Interpretation
**Case:** Arnold, Stoker, Giles, Nicolau  
**Why:** Investigators must separate what they observed from what they concluded. All cases require distinguishing fact from interpretation. Nicolau emphasizes that wet soil (observation) ≠ cause of movement (interpretation).

### Rule #2: Evidence ≠ Conclusion
**Case:** Arnold, Stoker, Menchaca, Nicolau  
**Why:** Evidence supports conclusions, but must be documented and traceable. Cases require evidence-based reasoning. Nicolau: Expert opinion is evidence, not the final conclusion; insurer remains responsible for evaluating evidence quality.

### Rule #3: Suspicion ≠ Evidence
**Case:** Arnold, Stoker  
**Why:** Suspicions don't justify denials. Documented facts do. Both cases require real evidence, not speculation.

### Rule #4: Contradiction ≠ Deception
**Case:** Giles, Menchaca  
**Why:** Contradictions require investigation, not assumptions. Both cases protect claimants from assumed intent.

### Rule #5: Intent ≠ Motive
**Case:** Arnold, Giles  
**Why:** Motive doesn't prove intent. Both cases distinguish between observable facts and assumptions about intent.

### Rule #6: Possibility ≠ Probability ≠ Proof
**Case:** Arnold, Stoker, Giles  
**Why:** Determinations must match evidence quality. All cases require clarity about certainty level.

### Rule #7: Recommendation ≠ Decision
**Case:** Stoker, Giles, Menchaca  
**Why:** Investigators recommend findings; decision-makers decide coverage. All cases protect investigator/decision-maker boundaries.

### Rule #8: Finding ≠ Allegation
**Case:** Arnold, Stoker, Giles  
**Why:** Findings are verified; allegations are not. Cases require investigators to clearly distinguish between the two.

### Rule #9: Missing Evidence ≠ Concealment
**Case:** Giles, Menchaca  
**Why:** Absence of evidence is not evidence of deception. Both cases protect investigators from overinterpreting gaps.

### Rule #10: Policy Language ≠ Interpretation
**Case:** Stoker, Menchaca  
**Why:** Policy language must be quoted or precisely cited. Cases prohibit misrepresenting what policy says.

### Rule #11: Source Attribution Required
**Case:** Arnold, Stoker, Giles, Menchaca  
**Why:** ALL cases require documenting where facts come from. This is the foundation of all four standards.

## Timeline of Standards (Chronological)

```
1987: Arnold established "reasonable documented basis" standard
      → Investigation must be documented, fact-based, traceable

1995: Stoker clarified coverage denial requirements
      → Denial must have valid contractual reason
      → Reason must be documented

1997: Giles refined claims handling standards  
      → Must pay promptly once liability is "reasonably clear"
      → Cannot delay with unnecessary document requests

2018: Menchaca unified contract and statutory violations
      → Investigation must be reasonable
      → Explanation must be clear
      → Decision must be timely
      → Policy language must be accurate
```

|-------|----------|----------|
| **Investigation** | Arnold | Is the report documented, fact-based, and traceable? |
| **Investigation** | Menchaca | Is the investigation reasonable and thorough? |
| **Denial Decision** | Stoker | Is the denial based on a valid contractual reason? |
| **Payment Decision** | Giles | Is payment timely after liability becomes clear? |

## Practical Application

### Example: Water Damage Claim

**1. Arnold** (Investigation phase)
- Have you documented everything with sources?
- Is your reasoning traceable from facts to conclusion?

**2. Menchaca** (Investigation phase)
- Have you conducted a reasonable investigation?
- Has the claimant had opportunity to respond?

**3. Stoker** (Denial decision)
- If denying: Is there a valid contractual reason?
- Is that reason documented?

**4. Giles** (Payment decision)
- If covering: Is payment prompt after liability is clear?
- Are you requesting only necessary documents?

**5. Menchaca** (Explanation phase)
- Is the decision clearly explained?
- Have you quoted (not paraphrased) policy language?
- Is the decision timely?

## Red Flags Across All Standards

🚩 Facts without sources → Arnold violation  
🚩 Observations without separating interpretation → Arnold violation  
🚩 Denial without valid reason → Stoker violation  
🚩 Delay after liability is clear → Giles violation  
🚩 Unclear explanation of decision → Menchaca violation  
🚩 Misquoted policy language → Menchaca violation  
🚩 Investigation gaps → Menchaca violation  
🚩 Contradictions not investigated → Giles/Menchaca violation

## Self-Audit Checklist

Use this before finalizing any investigative report:

### Arnold Check (Documented Basis)
- [ ] Every fact has a source?
- [ ] Observations separated from interpretations?
- [ ] Reasoning is traceable?
- [ ] Conclusion supported by documented facts?

### Stoker Check (Valid Denial Reason)
- [ ] If denied, is there a valid contractual reason?
- [ ] Is that reason documented?
- [ ] Could reader verify the reason?

### Giles Check (Timely Payment)
- [ ] Investigation completed within reasonable time?
- [ ] Payment prompt after liability is clear?
- [ ] No unnecessary document requests?
- [ ] Claimant knows decision status and timeline?

### Menchaca Check (Clear Explanation)
- [ ] Investigation was reasonable (thorough + documented)?
- [ ] Explanation is clear (specific policy + facts)?
- [ ] Policy language quoted, not paraphrased?
- [ ] Decision made timely?
- [ ] Claimant notified of reasoning?

**If "No" to any question, revise before finalizing.**

## Authority Hierarchy (For Your Reference)

When writing your investigative report, remember:

1. **Highest:** Texas court decisions (these four cases)
2. **High:** Texas Insurance Code § 21.21 (unfair settlement practices)
3. **Medium:** Company SOP and policy terms
4. **Medium:** Professional investigative standards
5. **Lower:** Industry best practices and peer examples

Your report must comply with all levels, starting with the cases above.

## Research Your Jurisdiction

These four standards apply in **Texas**. Insurance law varies by state.

If investigating claims in other states:
- Consult state-specific case law
- Review state Insurance Code
- Verify whether your jurisdiction recognizes "bad faith" tort
- Understand state-specific "reasonable investigation" standards

Same principles apply everywhere: Document. Source. Reason. Separate fact from conclusion.

But specific legal standards may differ.

## Bottom Line

All four cases say the same thing in different ways:

**Investigate thoroughly. Document everything. Separate fact from conclusion. Explain clearly. Decide timely. Quote policy accurately.**

A report that passes all four standards:
- ✅ Survives appellate review
- ✅ Protects investigator professionally
- ✅ Protects claimant fairly
- ✅ Serves organization's interests

A report that violates these standards:
- ❌ Vulnerable to bad faith liability
- ❌ Exposes investigator to challenge
- ❌ Creates claimant hardship
- ❌ Risks organizational sanctions

Choose the first path.
