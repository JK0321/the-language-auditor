# ACFE Alignment: Professional Language Rules

This document maps the 11 professional language rules to ACFE (Association of Certified Fraud Examiners) standards for fraud examination reporting.

**Key insight:** Your 11 rules are not invented standards—they **embody ACFE professional best practices** through the lens of professional language discipline.

## Rule-by-Rule Analysis

### Rule 1: Observation ≠ Interpretation

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Direct alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** Layer Separation (Principle #2)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Distinguish: observation, analysis, conclusion"

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE's Layer Separation principle requires that fraud examination reports clearly separate:
- What was directly observed or documented
- What analysis was performed
- What conclusions were reached

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "The claimant's story was fabricated."
- ✅ "The claimant stated [quote]. Documentation shows [facts]. The discrepancy between statement and documentation is [specific]."

**Authority:** ACFE Fraud Examination Handbook; professional reporting standards.

---

### Rule 2: Evidence ≠ Conclusion

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Direct alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** Evidence Anchoring (Principle #1)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Every material statement must be supported by relevant, reliable, and sufficient evidence."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE requires that every conclusion in a fraud examination report is traceable to documented evidence. The investigator reports evidence first, then draws conclusions only when evidence sufficiently supports them.

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "The subject manipulated records."
- ✅ "Transaction records were modified after initial entry, as reflected in system audit logs dated March 3 and March 5. This modification is consistent with [possible explanations]."

**Authority:** ACFE Fraud Examination Handbook; evidence standards.

---

### Rule 3: Suspicion ≠ Evidence

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Direct alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** Evidence Anchoring (Principle #1)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Suspicions do not justify findings. Only documented facts do."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE explicitly prohibits basing conclusions on suspicion, gut feeling, or circumstantial pattern-matching without documented evidence.

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "I suspected the loss report was inflated. My investigation determined it was fraud."
- ✅ "The loss report claimed [amount]. Investigation found [documented facts]. Discrepancy: [specific]. This suggests possible overstatement. Additional evidence needed."

**Authority:** ACFE Fraud Examination Handbook; professional skepticism requirement.

---

### Rule 4: Possibility ≠ Probability ≠ Proof

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Indirect (Supported)**

**ACFE Principle:** Controlled Uncertainty (Principle #3)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Do not overstate certainty. Explicitly state limits and unknowns."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE requires fraud examiners to be precise about certainty levels. A fact can be possible (could happen), probable (likely based on evidence), or proven (meets legal threshold).

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "This proves the scheme."
- ✅ "The evidence is consistent with unauthorized payments. The investigation did not determine the full extent of the activity."

**Authority:** ACFE Controlled Uncertainty principle; professional reporting standards.

---

### Rule 5: Intent ≠ Motive

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Direct alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** No Mental-State Assertions Without Proof (Principle #6)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Do not assert intent, motive, or knowledge unless proven with higher evidentiary threshold."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE strongly enforces this distinction. Motive (why someone had financial or emotional pressure) is not the same as intent (deliberate choice to commit fraud). Many fraud findings fail because investigators confuse the two.

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "The subject's financial need proves intent to commit fraud."
- ✅ "The subject's financial records show [facts]. Financial motive exists: [documented]. However, financial motive alone does not establish intent to commit fraud. Evidence of deliberate misrepresentation would be [specific facts]."

**Authority:** ACFE strongly enforces this; common source of failed fraud determinations.

---

### Rule 6: Contradiction ≠ Deception

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Direct alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** Neutrality and Completeness (Principle #5)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Include inculpatory evidence, exculpatory evidence, and contradictions. Do not resolve contradictions without evidence."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE requires that fraud examination reports document all contradictions and competing explanations. Contradictions are observable facts. Deception is inferred intent—which requires proof.

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "The contradictory statements prove the claimant was being deceptive."
- ✅ "Statement 1 [documented]. Statement 2 [documented]. Contradiction: [specific]. This inconsistency could indicate [possibilities]. Additional evidence to determine intent: [what would distinguish]."

**Authority:** ACFE Neutrality requirement; prevents bias-driven conclusions.

---

### Rule 7: Recommendation ≠ Decision

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Indirect (Strongly implied)**

**ACFE Principle:** Professional role boundaries

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Investigators report findings. Decision-makers determine outcomes."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE fraud examination standards implicitly require investigators to stay in role. The investigator's job is to report findings based on evidence. Coverage decisions, liability determinations, and prosecution recommendations are made by other professionals.

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "I recommend we deny this claim." / "I recommend prosecution."
- ✅ "Findings: [documented evidence]. Recommendation for review: [what decision-maker needs to consider]."

**Authority:** ACFE scope of practice; professional role separation.

---

### Rule 8: Finding ≠ Allegation

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Direct alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** Allegation Discipline (Principle #4)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Separate: allegation, suspicion, evidence, finding. Each requires different treatment."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE explicitly requires fraud examination reports to distinguish:
- **Allegations:** Claims made by others (not yet verified)
- **Suspicions:** Investigator's concerns (not yet supported)
- **Evidence:** Documented facts (observed or collected)
- **Findings:** Facts the investigation established (verified)

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "Finding: The claimant committed fraud."
- ✅ "Allegation: Management alleged fraudulent activity on [date]. Finding: Investigation examined [documents]. Evidence established: [facts]. Conclusion: [what findings prove/don't prove]."

**Authority:** ACFE Allegation Discipline principle; prevents bias contamination.

---

### Rule 9: Missing Evidence ≠ Concealment

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Direct alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** Scope and Limitation Transparency (Principle #7)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Document what was examined, what was not available, and impact of gaps."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE requires investigators to distinguish between "couldn't find" (evidence was absent or unavailable) and "evidence of hiding" (deliberate concealment was documented).

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "Records were missing, indicating concealment."
- ✅ "Requested records were not available. The investigation could not determine the reason for their absence."

**Authority:** ACFE prevents overreach in conclusions; distinguishes absence from misconduct.

---

### Rule 10: Policy Language ≠ Interpretation

**ACFE Status:** ⚠️ **Weak alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** Not explicitly in ACFE scope (legal layer)

**Why This Is Different:**

This rule operates in the legal/coverage domain, not strictly in fraud examination methodology. ACFE's scope is how to investigate and report on fraud facts. Policy interpretation belongs to legal specialists.

**ACFE Relationship:**

ACFE does require that investigators quote or precisely cite policy language when referring to it. But policy interpretation is deferred to legal reviewers.

**How It Applies:**

Investigators working in insurance must distinguish:
- What the policy language actually says (quote it)
- What the investigator interprets it to mean (note as interpretation)
- What legal specialist will determine it means (their job)

**Authority:** ACFE by implication (don't misquote policy); primarily a legal layer rule.

---

### Rule 11: Source Attribution Required

**ACFE Status:** ✅ **Strong alignment**

**ACFE Principle:** Evidence Anchoring (Principle #1) + Scope Transparency (Principle #7)

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Every material fact must have a source. Workpapers must be traceable to evidence."

**How ACFE Reinforces This Rule:**

ACFE makes workpaper traceability mandatory. Every statement in a fraud examination report must be traceable back to documentation in the investigation file. An independent reviewer should be able to verify every material fact.

Example ACFE requirement:
- ❌ "The claimant made inconsistent statements."
- ✅ "Claimant statement on [date] to [person]: '[quote].' Later statement on [date] to [person]: '[quote].' Inconsistency: [specific]."

**Authority:** ACFE workpaper standards; core requirement for all fraud examination reports.

## ACFE-Only Requirements (Not Explicitly in Your 11 Rules)

The ACFE research identified 4 principles worth considering for Phase 2 enhancement:

### Rule 12: Sufficiency Standard

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Evidence must be sufficient such that a reasonable professional could reach the same conclusion."

**When This Matters:** When your evidence supports a conclusion, it should be sufficient that another investigator reviewing your work would reach the same conclusion.

---

### Rule 13: Scope Transparency

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Reports must explicitly state limitations and gaps."

**When This Matters:** Readers need to know what the investigation covered and what it didn't. Undisclosed limitations undermine credibility.

---

### Rule 14: Red Flag ≠ Evidence

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Indicators are not proof. Red flags trigger investigation; they don't conclude it."

**When This Matters:** Just because a risk indicator is present doesn't mean fraud occurred.

---

### Rule 15: Evidence Must Be Traceable to Source

**ACFE Standard:**
> "Ability to reconstruct investigation from workpapers."

**When This Matters:** Reviewers (lawyers, auditors) must be able to follow the investigative chain of logic.

## Synthesis: How ACFE + Your Rules Work Together

**ACFE defines the METHOD:** How to investigate correctly (evidence quality, neutrality, documentation)

**Your 11 rules define the DISCIPLINE OF EXPRESSION:** How to write about what you found (language precision, logical flow, professional boundaries)

**Texas case law defines the CONSEQUENCES:** What happens if you don't do it right (bad faith liability, reasonable basis requirements)

When all three are aligned, a report becomes structurally defensible, legally sound, and professionally credible.
