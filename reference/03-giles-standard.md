# Giles Standard: "Reasonably Clear" Liability

**Case:** Universe Life Insurance Co. v. Giles (Texas Supreme Court, 1997)  
**Citation:** 881 S.W.2d 44  
**Key Standard:** An insurer breaches its duty of good faith when it delays or denies payment of a claim after its liability has become "reasonably clear."

---

## What the Court Said

An insurer cannot delay or deny a covered claim once it becomes "reasonably clear" that liability exists.

The standard is not "provable beyond doubt." The standard is "reasonably clear."

---

## What This Means for Investigators

Investigation must be PROMPT and THOROUGH.

Once a claim becomes "reasonably clear" as covered:
- Further delays are problematic
- Requests for unnecessary documentation are problematic
- Lost paperwork becomes problematic
- Repeated requests for the same information become problematic

"Reasonably clear" means: A reasonable investigator, with the evidence then available, would conclude the claim is covered.

---

## The Giles Facts

Ida Giles had heart bypass surgery shortly after purchasing health insurance from Universe Life.

Universe denied the claim on grounds of pre-existing condition.

Universe's reasons for denial:
- Medical records showed "history of chest pain"
- Records indicated "positive history of heart disease"  
- She had been prescribed cholesterol medications
- She had atherosclerosis

When Giles's physicians clarified:
- "Chest pain history" was a transcription error; she'd had chest pain only 2-3 weeks (AFTER policy issued)
- "Positive history of heart disease" meant family history, not personal history
- Cholesterol medications were for cholesterol, not heart condition
- She had no treatment for heart problems before the policy

**What Universe did:**
- Didn't question the physicians' credibility
- Didn't ask for clarification initially
- Kept denying the claim despite clarification
- Delayed 10 months before paying

**The court's ruling:** Once the physicians clarified the medical records (making coverage "reasonably clear"), Universe should have paid promptly. The delay of 10 months was bad faith.

## "Reasonably Clear" Analysis

### What Makes a Claim "Reasonably Clear" as Covered?

When the investigator, with available evidence, would conclude:
1. The loss type is covered (not excluded)
2. The policy is valid and in force
3. The premium was paid
4. The loss occurred during the coverage period
5. The claim meets policy conditions
6. Documentation is sufficient to verify coverage

### What Does NOT Make It "Reasonably Clear"?

- Theoretical argument from the claimant
- Claimant's assertion (without documentation)
- Preliminary investigation only (if more investigation is needed)
- Ambiguous policy language (without legal interpretation)

### What DOES Make It "Reasonably Clear"?

- Multiple confirmations of key facts
- Documentation from independent sources (not just claimant)
- Expert clarification of ambiguous information
- Policy language clearly supports coverage
- No valid exclusion applies

## Common Violations (Giles Standard)

### Violation 1: Delays After Reasonably Clear Liability

❌ **PROBLEM:** Claim becomes clearly covered. Investigator continues requesting documentation already provided.

Example:
- Day 1: Claimant submits medical records showing coverage
- Day 15: Investigator requests "additional documentation"
- Day 30: Claimant resubmits same documents
- Day 45: Investigator requests "clarification"
- Day 60: Claim still not paid

When liability became reasonably clear: Day 1-5 (after initial review)  
When delays became problematic: Day 15+

✅ **CORRECT:** 
- Day 1: Receive documentation
- Day 5: Initial review confirms coverage appears clear
- Day 8: Request any REMAINING documentation (not duplicates)
- Day 12: All information received
- Day 14: DECISION MADE and paid, or specific reason stated if claim denied

### Violation 2: Requesting Unnecessary Documentation

❌ **PROBLEM:** Investigator requests documents that don't change coverage determination.

"Please provide receipts for all repair costs" when policy limits, not actual repair costs, determine payout.

✅ **CORRECT:** Only request documentation that affects coverage decision:
- "To determine if coverage applies, please provide: [specific documents that would change decision]"
- "We have reviewed your claim and coverage is clear for [amount]. Here is our decision."

### Violation 3: Lost or Mishandled Paperwork

❌ **PROBLEM:** Claimant submits documentation. Insurer loses it. Claimant resubmits. Process repeats.

After "reasonably clear" point, further delays from insurer error become bad faith.

✅ **CORRECT:** 
- Document receipt of each submission
- Track chain of custody
- Confirm to claimant what was received and when
- If documents are lost, acknowledge error and process immediately upon resubmission

### Violation 4: Failure to Provide Explanation of Denial

❌ **PROBLEM:** Claim is denied without explanation of why coverage doesn't apply.

"Your claim has been denied. Please contact us for details."

✅ **CORRECT:** Once "reasonably clear" determination is made (coverage applies OR doesn't apply), provide:
- "Coverage determination: COVERED" OR "Coverage determination: DENIED"
- Specific policy section
- Specific facts that triggered coverage
- If denied: Specific exclusion that applies

## Timeline of Giles Violations

| Timeline | Action | Status | Problem |
|----------|--------|--------|---------|
| Day 1-5 | Receive claim + documentation | Reasonable review | Acceptable delay |
| Day 5 | Liability becomes "reasonably clear" | Coverage is clear | Investigators should know |
| Day 5-10 | Request essential remaining info | Reasonable | Acceptable |
| Day 10 | All essential info received | Liability fully clear | No excuse for further delay |
| Day 10-20 | Request unnecessary duplication | Problematic | Violates Giles |
| Day 20+ | Still not paid despite clear liability | Violation | Bad faith |

---

## Red Flags (Giles Violations)

🚩 Claim marked "covered" but payment delayed weeks with no explanation  
🚩 Requesting same document multiple times  
🚩 Asking for "additional information" when initial docs already establish coverage  
🚩 Processing takes longer than documented investigation requires  
🚩 Claimant not notified of coverage determination timeline  
🚩 Lost documents not immediately reprocessed  
🚩 Denial issued without explanation of policy/fact basis

## Self-Test: Does Your Claim Handling Meet Giles Standard?

For claims you determine are covered:

1. ✅ When did liability become "reasonably clear"?
   - [ ] Day [X] - specific event: [describe]
   - Or [ ] Never (still investigating)

2. ✅ If clearly covered, when was it paid?
   - [ ] Within [X] days of becoming clear
   - [ ] Still delayed; reason: [state specific reason]

3. ✅ Did you request only necessary documentation?
   - [ ] Yes, only: [list specific documents needed]
   - [ ] No, I may have requested: [duplicates/unnecessary items]

4. ✅ Was your decision documented and explained?
   - [ ] Yes, to claimant: [explain what we said]
   - [ ] No, claimant doesn't have full explanation

If liability is "reasonably clear" but claim remains unpaid, Giles may apply. Resolve quickly or provide clear reason for continued investigation.

## Giles vs Arnold vs Stoker

| Standard | Focus | Applies When |
|----------|-------|---|
| **Arnold** | Investigation quality | Report is written |
| **Stoker** | Denial validity | Claim is denied |
| **Giles** | Payment timeliness | Liability is reasonably clear |

All three can apply to the same claim.

---

## Key Takeaway

Giles protects claimants from delay tactics.

Once an investigator determines "liability is reasonably clear" (coverage applies):
- Payment should follow promptly
- No more unnecessary document requests
- No delays without explanation
- Explanation of coverage decision must be provided

Giles doesn't eliminate reasonable investigation.

Giles eliminates UNREASONABLE delays after investigation is complete and coverage is clear.
