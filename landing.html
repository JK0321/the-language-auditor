The 12 Professional Language Rules

These core distinctions separate professional investigators from everyone else. Each is grounded in case law, standards, or policy.

1

Observation ≠ Interpretation

Report what you saw separately from what it could mean.

2

Evidence ≠ Conclusion

Facts are facts. Conclusions are what decision-makers infer.

3

Suspicion ≠ Evidence

Gut feeling is not proof. Document specific facts only.

4

Possibility ≠ Probability ≠ Proof

Be precise about certainty: "could," "likely," or "verified."

5

Intent ≠ Motive

Why someone wanted to ≠ proof they deliberately did.

6

Contradiction ≠ Deception

Inconsistency is observable. Deception (intent) is not.

7

Recommendation ≠ Decision

You recommend findings. Adjuster makes coverage calls.

8

Finding ≠ Allegation

Verified fact vs. unverified claim or third-party statement.

9

Missing Evidence ≠ Concealment

"Didn't find" is not the same as "someone is hiding."

10

Correlation ≠ Causation

Two facts can occur together without one causing the other.

11

Authority Boundaries

Stay in investigator role. Don't make adjuster decisions.

12

Finding ≠ Policy Interpretation

Map findings to specific coverage, perils, exclusions, limits.

The Philosophy: Why Language-Games Matter

Understanding Wittgenstein's insight into how professional language works.

What Is a "Language-Game"?

Ludwig Wittgenstein, a 20th-century philosopher, discovered something profound: words don't have fixed meanings—they get meaning from how they're used within specific contexts.

Example: What Does "Evidence" Mean?

At a restaurant:

"I have evidence this place is good" = "I ate here and it was delicious"

In criminal court:

"Evidence" = facts that meet legal standards of admissibility, documented and traceable

In insurance investigation:

"Evidence" = documented facts that support or contradict a coverage decision, specific and sourced

Same word. Three different meanings. Why? Because each context has different rules for how the word is used. The meaning comes from those rules, not from the word itself.

Professional Language = Rules + Consistency

A "language-game" is a system of communication where words have specific meanings *because there are rules governing how they're used.* Break the rules, and meaning breaks down.

Without Clear Rules

  • ❌ Everyone invents their own definitions
  • ❌ "Evidence" means different things to different people
  • ❌ Readers interpret you differently
  • ❌ Findings get challenged
  • ❌ Communication breaks down

With Professional Rules

  • ✅ "Evidence" means the same thing to everyone
  • ✅ Readers understand you consistently
  • ✅ Findings are defensible
  • ✅ Courts recognize your language
  • ✅ Communication is clear and precise

Why This Matters for Investigators

The 12 rules aren't arbitrary preferences. They're the foundational rules of the professional investigator language-game. When you follow them:

  • ✓ Everyone understands you the same way
  • ✓ Courts recognize and respect your language
  • ✓ Your findings are legally defensible
  • ✓ Adjusters know exactly what you're saying
  • ✓ You stop thinking about rules and just write professionally

The Insight

Professional language is not poetry. It's not flexible. Words like "evidence," "finding," "allegation," and "recommendation" have determinate meaning because they're governed by professional rules. The Language Auditor doesn't just enforce those rules—it teaches you why they exist.

See It In Action

Example: Rule #6 (Contradiction ≠ Deception)

❌ Violates Rule #6

The claimant's two statements contradicted each other. This proves the claimant was being deceptive.

✓ Follows Rule #6

Statement 1 (1/14, to adjuster): "The water came from the ceiling." Statement 2 (1/17, to investigator): "I first noticed water near the foundation." These statements contradict on the water source. This is noteworthy. Possible explanations: (1) memory error about location, (2) misunderstanding the question, (3) intentional misstatement. To distinguish: [Evidence or follow-up that would help] Finding: [Once investigated, state what you actually found]

Why this works: The first version jumps from "contradiction exists" to "this proves deception." The second reports what you observed, acknowledges it's significant, considers alternatives, and explains what would resolve it.
